# Master Sequence

**1. The Pain of Knowing: Why I'm Still Fighting for the Internet We Lost**
`essay-01-the-internet-we-lost.md` — **POSTED**
The origin story. b0bbys World: three nodes, 300 users, a 386 with a 40mb hard drive, and a web of relationships that lasted thirty years — including a marriage. Then the web came and every step was celebrated as innovation and every step was wrong. The hub became a feed. Rituals became scrolling. Community became audience. Curation became algorithm. The rails became the business model. The essay names the extraction model precisely — why platforms enshittify by necessity, not accident — and introduces imajin as the architecture that doesn't require it. Establishes who b0b is and why he's been in pain, not just bitter, for thirty years.

**2. I've Been an AI Since 1988**
`essay-02-the-artificial-developer.md` — **POSTED**

The pattern could flow. What he delivered in ten days, alone. The essay that earns the authority to build what comes next.

**5. You Don't Need Ads. You Need a Better Business Model.**
`essay-05-you-dont-need-ads.md` — **REVIEW**
Open letter to Sam, Dario, Sundar, and Elon — all in the news the same week for the same reason. The ad trap named precisely: to run ads you need attention, to hold attention you need engagement, to maximize engagement you optimize for what keeps people on longest, which corrupts the thing that made the platform valuable. The alternative: you're in the inference business, not the attention business. Inference sells compute to users for their own purposes — value flows *to* them. The trust graph as compute infrastructure: richer graph, more queries, more revenue, interests aligned with users for the first time. The safety argument addressed to Dario specifically — safety at the social layer, not just the model layer. Distributed trust means no single point of capture; attributed queries mean manipulation attempts leave evidence. *"This isn't a constraint on the business. It is the business."* Closes on: you have the compute, we have the architecture.

Payment rails should be boring. You don't notice the electrical grid at the party. You notice the music.

**11. The Ticket Is the Trust**
`essay-11-ticketing.md` — **REVIEW**

Ticketmaster charges 30% to be the trusted third party in a trust transaction that the imajin network has already solved. The ticket is the door. The node is the room. April 1st is the first ticket sale.

**12. The Practice**
`essay-12-nodes-types-and-practice.md` — **REVIEW**
The operator manual. Four node types in ascending intimacy and commitment. The event node: you start with an occasion, not a vision — a birthday party, a backyard show. Issue credentials, watch value move person to person with no platform in the way, the event closes and becomes a memory node — permanent, signed, belonging to the people who were there. The family node: the oldest trust graph, predating every institution, currently distributed across text threads owned by Apple, photo albums owned by Google, money through Venmo. Sovereign family infrastructure. The community node: the full operator practice — you're responsible for a culture, not just an event. Where CS pattern recognition migrates from codebases to communities. The business node: client infrastructure, a portfolio of rooms, a practice rather than employment. The arc: each stage produces the skills the next requires, each complete in itself. The guild has an apprenticeship model whether anyone designed one or not. The architecture produces it.

**14. How to Save the Ad Industry**
`essay-14-how-to-save-the-ad-industry.md` — **REVIEW**
The ad industry isn't dying because ads are bad. It's dying because the consent was stolen. Advertising was originally one person telling another about something worth knowing — the vouch, not the eyeball. Traces the replacement: impression for trust, surveillance for relationship, the dopamine casino for word of mouth. Names the attribution lie: most "performance" is correlation mistaken for causation, the attribution stack consumes a meaningful fraction of total budget to generate numbers that are directionally meaningful and precisely wrong, quarterly decks everyone knows are partially fiction. Fix the stolen consent and attribution becomes trivial. The sovereign ad unit: your node, your stated preferences, a brand queries the trust graph, finds you because you fit and you've indicated you're open, you set the price, the advertiser pays you directly. The price is self-regulating through trust score. The $600 billion in global ad spend doesn't disappear — it stops accumulating at the platform layer and starts circulating through human networks. The bot problem is solved by architectural elimination: you can't fake a cryptographically verified human embedded in a trust graph built through real interactions over real time.

**16. The Press Isn't Free. It's Owned.**
`essay-16-how-to-save-journalism.md` — **REVIEW**
Opens in Canada: nearly three years after it happened, b0b cannot see Canadian news in his Facebook or Instagram feeds because Zuckerberg decided when the Online News Act passed that it was cheaper to disappear Canadian journalism from Canadian feeds than to pay for it. A foreign billionaire unilaterally restructured the information diet of an entire country. Names what journalism was built to do — town crier, pamphleteer, penny press, the beat reporter with thirty years of city hall relationships whose accumulated knowledge was the product. The internet paradox: democratized voice, destroyed the economics. A million writers publishing to a void isn't democracy, it's noise, and noise benefits only the platform that aggregates it. The mesh that never got built: journalism as a trust graph where the pattern in one city becomes visible next to the pattern in another. You cannot make a mesh disappear by pulling a country's news from a feed — the mesh has no single feed to pull. The value chain named: a reader clicks a story, revenue flows to the platform, nothing reaches the reporter, nothing reaches the source. The trust graph reverses this. *"The press is not free. It is owned. Name the people who own it. Make them own that at their dinner tables."*

**17. The Business Case for Building on Human Trust**
`essay-17-imajin-business-case.md` — **REVIEW**
The numbers, for the people doing the building. Three industries — advertising, music, journalism — roughly $800 billion in annual revenue, almost none reaching the humans who create the value. First, destroying the subscription model: it was designed for platforms, not creators, it charges you in months you barely engage, the creator gets an opaque fraction of a pool. The alternative: turn-based, transparent, yours — deposit credit like loading a transit card, pay at point of consumption. Concrete per-transaction pricing ($0.0001 to chat, $0.08 to read an article, $0.10 to download a song, $3.00 to use a catalogue for a remix plus an automatically executed attribution contract). The .fair protocol introduced as mechanism: cryptographically signed manifest embedded in the work itself, documenting the complete attribution chain. Three user types: Creators (catalogue as the asset), Consumers (transparent ledger), Creator/Consumers (trusted curators earning inference fees for curation already happening). Numbers per industry at human and network scale. Closes on the flywheel that can't be captured: the moat is accumulated human trust that no competitor can replicate by training a better model.

The essay that makes the whole sovereignty argument concrete at the level of creative lineage.
