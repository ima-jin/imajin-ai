# Imajin — Business Case for Muskoka

**Your reputation. Your customers. Your data. Not theirs.**

---

## The Problem Every Local Business Knows

You built your business on relationships. Word of mouth. Regulars who come back every season. The family that's been eating at your restaurant for 20 years.

Then the platforms arrived.

- **Google** owns your reviews. Change their algorithm? You disappear.
- **Yelp** holds your reputation hostage. One bad review from a tourist can tank your season.
- **Uber Eats** takes 30% of every order. You do the cooking. They take the cut.
- **Eventbrite** charges up to 9.9% per ticket. For software that sends an email.
- **Booking software** charges $50–$500/month. For a calendar.

And every dollar you pay these platforms? It leaves Canada. Stripe, Google, Yelp, Square, Eventbrite, Uber — all headquartered in San Francisco. A Muskoka restaurant paying $25,000–$50,000/year in platform and software fees is sending that money directly to Silicon Valley. Multiply by every business in the region and it's **millions of dollars leaving the local economy annually** — for services that don't cook, clean, build, or serve anyone.

**The worst part?** If you leave any of these platforms, you leave empty-handed. Your reviews, your customer list, your booking history — it all stays on their servers.

---

## What Imajin Does

Imajin is Canadian-built, open-source software that lets businesses own their digital infrastructure instead of renting it from foreign platforms.

### 1. Your Customers Build Your Presence — For Free

Here's the part that changes everything: **businesses don't have to sign up first. Their customers do it for them.**

A customer with the Imajin app visits a restaurant, checks in, leaves a review. That creates a business record on the network — unclaimed, community-built. Other customers check in too. Reviews accumulate. A reputation forms.

When the business owner is ready, they claim their record and inherit everything — reviews, customer connections, check-in history. **The business didn't have to do anything to get started.** Their customers built their presence for them.

This means adoption isn't "convince 200 businesses to sign up for new software." It's "get customers using the app, and the businesses follow naturally."

### 2. Every Transaction Becomes a Relationship

Right now, a tourist pays with a credit card, leaves Muskoka, and the business has nothing. No email. No way to reach them next season. The relationship evaporated at the register.

With Imajin, when a customer pays — for a meal, a ticket, a cottage rental — they verify their email to get the receipt. That's it. Now the business has a **direct, permanent connection** to that customer. No Mailchimp subscription. No Instagram algorithm deciding whether they see your post next spring. No platform between you and the person who already chose you.

Every transaction is a relationship you own. Not a receipt that disappears.

### 3. Your Reviews Belong to You

Today, reviews live on Google's servers. Imajin treats reviews as **signed records from verified customers** — real people, with real identity, attached to your business permanently.

- Reviews travel with your business. Leave Google? Your reviews come with you.
- **Bad actors get less weight.** A reviewer who leaves 1-star reviews everywhere gradually loses influence. No moderation team needed. The math handles it.
- **Good reviewers matter more.** A local regular who's reviewed 50 businesses thoughtfully? Their word carries real weight. Trust is earned, not gamed.

### 4. Eliminate the Platform Tax

You'll still accept credit cards — Visa and Mastercard still get their processing fee. What disappears is **everything stacked on top**:

| Platform Layer | What They Charge | With Imajin |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Ticketing (Eventbrite, etc.) | Up to 9.9% per ticket | $0 — built in |
| Delivery apps (Uber Eats, etc.) | 15–30% per order | $0 — direct connection |
| Booking software | $50–$500/month | $0 — open source |
| Review site advertising | $300–$1,000/month | $0 — reviews are free |
| Email marketing (Mailchimp, etc.) | $20–$300/month | $0 — direct customer connections |

For a business currently paying for ticketing, booking, review management, and email marketing, that's **$5,000–$20,000/year back** — and that money stays in Muskoka instead of going to San Francisco.

### 5. Referral Chains That Actually Pay

Tourism is a referral economy. The cottage owner sends guests to the restaurant. The restaurant recommends the boat rental. The boat rental suggests the ice cream shop. Today, all of that value is invisible — a verbal "tell them I sent you" that no one tracks.

Imajin makes referrals trackable and compensable. When a cottage owner refers a guest to your restaurant, that referral is recorded. If the guest books, the referring business automatically earns a small cut. No manual tracking. No "did you mention my name?" at the register.

Multiple businesses can co-host events and split ticket revenue automatically — each business's share defined up front, settlement instant.

### 6. Seasonal Worker Reputation

Muskoka hires thousands of seasonal staff every year. A server who worked three summers at a resort has no portable proof of that — just a résumé and maybe a phone number for a reference.

With Imajin, each employer leaves a signed record on the worker's identity. Next season, the next employer sees a verified work history — not claims they have to call around to check. Good workers build portable reputation. Bad actors can't reinvent themselves every May.

### 7. Verifiable Local Credentials

"Muskoka Made." "Muskoka Business Association Member." "Health Inspection Passed." Today these are stickers and PDFs anyone can fake.

On Imajin, these become signed credentials attached to the business — verifiable by any customer, instantly, from their phone. Real trust, not marketing.

## How It Works (Non-Technical)

Think of it like email. Gmail and Outlook are different services, but they can send messages to each other because they follow the same rules (a protocol called SMTP).

Imajin defines the rules for how businesses and customers interact — identity, reviews, payments, trust — and any software that follows those rules can participate. No single company controls it. No one can shut it off.

**You don't need to understand the technology to use it.** Customers check in, pay, and review. Businesses claim their presence and own everything. The technology is invisible — the relationships are what matter.

Imajin is built for exactly this. Canadian software that works locally, that you own, that respects the relationships you've spent decades building.
