## The Coordination Tax

Here's what nobody talks about: most of what we do at work — most of what we do *period* — isn't the thing. It's coordinating about the thing.

Status meetings. Email threads. Slack pings. "Just checking in." "Per my last message." "Can you send me that doc?" "When are you free?" "What's the latest on...?"

We built entire industries around the fact that human brains can't stay in sync. And then we called those industries "productivity."

| What We Built | What It Actually Is |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Email | Asynchronous context transfer between brains |
| Calendars | Negotiating availability across trust boundaries |
| Project management (Jira, Asana) | Manually synchronizing state between humans |
| CRMs (Salesforce) | Outsourcing relationship memory to a database |
| Search (Google) | Asking an index because you can't ask a person |
| Meetings | Context synchronization rituals |
| Documentation (Confluence, Notion) | Write-once-read-never knowledge preservation |
| Recruiting (LinkedIn) | Dead résumé databases |
| Scheduling (Calendly) | Round-trip negotiation for a simple question |
| Legal discovery | Billing thousands to search through context |

Every one of these exists because of the same limitation: **humans can't efficiently share context across trust boundaries.**

That's it. That's the whole problem these trillion-dollar industries are solving.

## The Collapse

Now imagine every person has a presence. Not a chatbot. Not a profile page. A living, attributed, trust-gated representation of what they know and what they've done. Trained on their actual context — their work, their writing, their decisions, their values.

Your presence can answer "what's the status?" because it was there when the status changed.

Your presence can answer "when are you free?" because it knows your calendar and your preferences.

Your presence can answer "what would Ryan think about this?" because it's been learning what Ryan thinks for years.

And here's the key: **every query settles through .fair.** The person whose knowledge answered the question gets compensated. Not the platform that hosted the conversation. The actual human whose accumulated context made the answer possible.

When presences can talk to each other — trust-gated, human-routed, attributed at every hop — the coordination layer collapses.

Not gradually. Not "disrupted." **Collapses.**

| What Collapses | What Remains |
|---------------|-------------|
| Status meetings | Decisions (the human part) |
| Most email | Genuine correspondence |
| Project tracking | Actual project work |
| CRMs | Real relationships |
| Search-as-index | Ask-the-network |
| Scheduling tools | Showing up |
| Documentation wikis | Living knowledge |
| Recruiting platforms | Trust graph reputation |

The survivors are tools for **doing**, not **coordinating**. Code editors. Design tools. Musical instruments. Machines. Kitchen equipment. The things humans use to create.

Everything else was overhead.

## The Pattern

> TODO: Ryan — the communication pattern you saw with Steven. Human → AI (synthesize) → AI (summarize) → Human. Both humans stay in the loop. Both AIs handle the grunt work. The fees flow at every node.

The Human Router Protocol isn't just a patent. It's the fundamental architecture of how knowledge moves in a trust network.

A human sets intent. Their presence synthesizes it — structures, contextualizes, enriches with relevant memory. The message travels to another presence, which summarizes it for its human. The human responds. The cycle continues.

At every hop:
- The synthesizing presence gets attributed for its work
- The memory system that provided context gets attributed for its knowledge  
- The inference provider gets attributed for compute
- The platform takes a settlement fee
- The human who routed the message gets credited for their judgment

**Nobody in this chain is extracting.** Everyone is contributing. Everyone is compensated. The fees flow to the humans and systems that added signal, not to the platforms that hosted the pipe.
