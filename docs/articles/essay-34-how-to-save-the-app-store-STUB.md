## The Toll Booth

[EXPAND: Apple takes 30%. Google takes 30%. You build the app, find the users, run the servers — and a trillion-dollar company takes a third for the privilege of being listed. They call it distribution. It's a toll booth on a road you paved yourself.]

## The Real Cost Isn't Money

[EXPAND: The 30% is the visible tax. The invisible one is worse: every app on the store gives the platform complete user data. Downloads, usage patterns, purchase history, location. You built a fitness app — Apple now knows your users' health patterns. You built a messaging app — Google knows who talks to whom. The data flows up, never down.]

## The Kill Switch

[EXPAND: Parler. Fortnite. Any app that challenges the platform's interests gets removed. Not by users, not by the market — by a single company's policy team. Your business exists at the pleasure of someone else's terms of service. That's not distribution, it's tenancy.]

## What If Apps Could Read Proofs Instead of Data?

[EXPAND: An app on the Imajin network doesn't get user data. It gets chain proofs. "This DID is real. Has preliminary standing. $500 in verified settlements. Countersigned by 12 other DIDs." Everything you need to trust the user, nothing you don't need to surveil them.

The app imports @imajin/auth. Reads chains. Verifies attestations. Done. No data pipeline, no analytics SDK, no tracking pixels. The user's chain tells you they're trustworthy without telling you who they are.]

## The Userspace Is Disposable

[EXPAND: The kernel (auth + pay) is permanent. The userspace (every app) is replaceable. Don't like the marketplace? Build a better one. The chains don't care. The attestations survive. The trust graph persists. Apps come and go; the cryptographic substrate is permanent.

This is what Apple and Google fear most — not a competing store, but a world where the store doesn't matter. When identity and trust live in the user's chain, distribution is just discovery. And discovery doesn't require a 30% cut.]

## The Registry, Not the Landlord

[EXPAND: The registry is a phone book, not a gatekeeper. It helps you find apps. It doesn't control them, tax them, or kill them. An app joins the network by speaking the protocol. It leaves by... stopping. No approval process, no review board, no policy team deciding your business model is inconvenient.]

## The Developer Gets to Keep Their Business

[EXPAND: No revenue share. No data surrender. No kill switch. You build it, you run it, you keep it. The network provides identity, trust, and payments — the hard infrastructure problems. Everything else is yours.]
