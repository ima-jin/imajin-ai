A man burned down a 1.2 million square foot warehouse last week.

Kimberly-Clark distribution center in Ontario, California. Pallets of toilet paper. Six alarms. 175 firefighters. $600 million in damages. Before the building was fully consumed, he posted a video of himself lighting the pallets.

"All you had to do was pay us enough to live."

"Didn't see the shareholders picking up a shift."

He's been charged. He'll do time. The commentary is sorting into its predictable camps — working class hero, no he's not, capitalism did this, no he did this, sympathy and condemnation rotating on a twelve-hour cycle.

I don't want to adjudicate that. I want to talk about the boss.

Not Kimberly-Clark specifically. The boss I've met. The one I think you've met too.

---

I've worked for people who understood, at some structural level, that they were managing a negotiation between what their employees produced and what they paid out. And I've worked for people who didn't think of it as a negotiation at all — who experienced the surplus as simply theirs. Not by malice. By a set of assumptions so deep they never needed to be stated.

One of them bought expensive art for the office. Not cheap art. The kind you acquire because you are the kind of person who acquires it. The kind that hangs in spaces that communicate status to everyone who walks through.

The same person donated millions to charities. Real money. Real causes. His name on buildings. On programs. On things that would carry his identity forward into the world as evidence of his generosity.

He threw parties. He bought things. He cultivated a life that expressed, in every direction, that there was abundance here. That this was a place of plenty.

Meanwhile his employees were negotiating whether to keep their apartments.

The thing that still sits wrong with me is the charity piece.

Because the art — that's consumption. You can argue it's taste, status signaling, the specific narcissism of a certain class of wealthy person. It's not admirable but it's legible.

The charity is different. Charity is performing generosity with money you didn't share. It is deciding, on behalf of the people who generated the surplus, that the best use of their labor is your preferred cause. It is philanthropy as tax on your employees who never voted for the mission.

He was generous with everyone except the people in the room.

---

Someone threw a Molotov cocktail at Sam Altman's house.

OpenAI began as a nonprofit. The researchers who built GPT did so under the premise that this was for everyone. Then Altman restructured. The company that was supposed to be open became the most valuable closed system on Earth. The people who built the models watched the value of their work route upward into a structure they no longer controlled, toward a person they no longer trusted.

The researchers couldn't see where the value went. Couldn't audit the decisions. Couldn't exit with what they'd built. The options were the same ones they've always been: endure it, leave empty-handed, or break something.

Altman didn't buy art for the office. He didn't need to. The entire company was the art — a monument to the idea that one person's vision entitles them to the surplus of everyone else's labor. The charity version of this is the "open" in OpenAI itself: a word that once meant something, now performing generosity it no longer delivers.

The warehouse worker set $600 million on fire. The person who threw the Molotov aimed at a house. Different targets. Same accounting.

---

The warehouse is gone. Chamel Abdulkarim's coworkers are out of jobs. The people he claimed to speak for are the ones who lost their shifts, their income, their ability to pay the rent he was burning everything to protest. The act destroyed what it was trying to protect.

That's not a working class hero. That's what desperation looks like when the pressure valve has been welded shut long enough.

The OpenAI researchers who left didn't throw anything. They published letters. They testified. They started competing companies. The polite version of the same fracture. Different class, different tools, same structural position: people who built something they couldn't own, working inside an architecture that made their claim invisible.

But the act isn't the point. The conditions are.

"Didn't see the shareholders picking up a shift."

That's not rage. That's accounting.

---

Three stories. One pattern.

A boss buys art with surplus his employees generated. He puts his name on charities funded by their labor. The employees can see the distance between what they made and what they received, but there's nothing in the system that records their claim. The knowledge lives in their bodies. It accumulates.

A warehouse worker watches billions of dollars of product move through a building his labor makes possible. He can't see the ledger. He can't negotiate with the ledger. The ledger doesn't know he exists. He lights a match.

A CEO restructures a nonprofit into the most valuable private company in AI. The researchers who built the technology watch their work disappear behind a cap table they never signed. They can't fork the model. They can't prove what they contributed. Their names aren't on the weights.

Every extraction story is an infrastructure story.

The boss could buy the art because there was no ledger his employees could read. Altman could close OpenAI because the contributors had no ownership layer. The warehouse could pay poverty wages because the value chain was invisible.

You own the company. The surplus is yours. How you allocate it — to yourself, to your status objects, to your preferred causes, to your employees — is your prerogative. That's what ownership means.

The employees know a different story. They know what they made. They know what they were paid. The distance between those two numbers is legible, even without a spreadsheet. People know, in their bodies, when the accounting is off.

And when there's no infrastructure to honor the chain, that knowledge has nowhere to go.

Sometimes it goes sideways in a Signal thread nobody names.

Sometimes it stays in the body for decades, showing up as the specific bitterness of someone who gave everything and was never seen.

Sometimes it leaves politely and starts a competing company.

And sometimes, at 12:30am in a warehouse in Ontario, California, it lights a pallet on fire.

---

The fix is not charity.

The fix is not a bonus structure your employer controls and can revoke.

The fix is not a better boss.

The fix is not "open" in the name.

The fix is infrastructure. Infrastructure that makes the chain visible. That records who built what, who's owed what, and where the value went — not as a gift from the top, but as a property of how the system works. A receipt that follows every transaction. An attribution chain that can't be edited by the person who benefits most from your name not being on it.

"All you had to do was pay us enough to live."

That's not a radical demand. That's the minimum. And the reason it went unanswered — in the warehouse, in the office, in the nonprofit that became a $150 billion company — is that there was no layer of the system where the answer could be *required*.

That's the layer I'm building.
