## The Lemonade Stand

A kid sets up a lemonade stand. Charges a dollar a cup. The protocol takes a penny. The node — dad's laptop running in the garage — takes half a penny. The kid accrues a quarter penny in network equity. The neighborhood association takes whatever cut it wants on top.

Nobody asked permission. The kid booted a node, it generated its own identity, and started settling transactions. Sovereign from the first cup.

If the neighborhood association gets greedy — charges 15% because they "provide the foot traffic" — the kid unplugs and runs a sovereign lemonade network. Loses the foot traffic. Keeps the recipe. Keeps the identity. Keeps the transaction history.

That's the exit door. It's always open. And because it's always open, the neighborhood association has to compete on value, not captivity.

## The Four Layers

Every transaction has four fee layers:

| Layer | Who sets it | Range | Purpose |
|-------|------------|-------|---------|
| Protocol | MJN Foundation | 0.25% – 2% | Funds the network |
| Node | Whoever runs the machine | 0.25% – 2% | Funds the infrastructure |
| Buyer credit | Node operator | 0.25% – 2% | Rewards participation |
| Scope fee | The community | 0% – whatever | Funds the community |

Three layers are protocol-governed. Bounded. Nobody can zero them out (starvation prevention), nobody can jack them to predatory levels (capture prevention).

The fourth layer — the scope fee — is sovereign. Zero to whatever. Set by the community, the business, the family, the individual. Their economy. Their rules. Publicly advertised so everyone knows the deal before they transact.

At defaults, it's 2%. At minimum, 0.75%. Compared to Stripe's 2.9% + 30¢, this is already cheaper. And it funds four parties instead of one corporation.

## The Edge

Here's where it gets interesting.

The lemonade stands form a network. Ten kids on ten blocks, all peered, all settling through the mesh. They share reputation, share identity verification, share the economic graph. A customer verified on one stand is verified on all of them.

Down the street, there's a rave community. Different people, different economy. They sell tickets, book DJs, run a bar tab system. Their own scope, their own fee, their own nodes.

These two networks have nothing in common.

Until one of the lemonade kids starts going to raves. Or a DJ sets up a lemonade stand at the afterparty. One person, in both scopes, creates a peering point. A transaction crosses the boundary. The scope fees split. And suddenly two completely unrelated economies are settling through each other.

Nobody designed this. Nobody approved it. Nobody even planned it. It emerged from the edge — from the overlap between two communities that happened to share one member.

## The Mesh

Now multiply. The ravers peer with a venue community. The venues peer with coffee shops. The coffee shops peer with a farmers' market. The farmers peer with an ag cooperative. The cooperative peers with a rural education network.

Each peering point is a person — or a node — that exists in both scopes. The transaction crosses the boundary, the fees split, the identities verify across the gap. The more connections, the more valuable the mesh. Not because someone built a platform. Because people joined communities and those communities found each other.

This is how the internet actually grew. Not from a master plan. From peering agreements between networks that had local incentives to connect. BGP — the protocol that routes internet traffic — doesn't know what's in the packets. It just routes. MJN doesn't know what's in the transactions. It just settles.

The protocol is the highway. It doesn't need to know what's in the trucks.

## The Choice

Every node gets the same choice at boot:

1. **Genesis.** No parent. You are the platform. Your relay DID is your identity, your settlement address, your network of one. You grow from zero.

2. **Peer.** Find the mesh. Connect. Collect node fees. Share scope fees on cross-platform transactions. Gain access to every identity, every reputation, every service on the network.

3. **Stay sovereign.** Run your own thing. Platform-to-platform peering only — one edge, one connection point. Full independence. Less connectivity.

The economic gravity pulls toward peering. More users, more services, more settlements, more MJN accrual. But sovereignty is always an option. And the option being there is what keeps the mesh honest.

Nobody is locked in. Everybody is incentivized. The lock-in is the value, not the contract.
